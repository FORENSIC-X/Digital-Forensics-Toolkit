## Case Background

A whistleblower complaint was filed against a senior data analyst at a Bengaluru-based fintech startup, alleging insider trading. The suspect allegedly photographed confidential quarterly financial projections from the company's internal dashboard before the results were made public, and shared them with an external stockbroker.

The suspect was placed under surveillance on **April 10, 2026**. Digital evidence was seized from his workstation and personal laptop on **April 14, 2026**.

Two image files were recovered from a hidden folder on his personal laptop:

- `dashboard_snap_1.jpg`
- `dashboard_snap_2.jpg`

Both files were found with their Windows file timestamps showing **April 13, 2026** — the night before the seizure.

## The Critical Finding — Timestomping Detected

Comparing the timestamp layers across both files reveals a forensically impossible contradiction:

| File | FileCreateDate (OS) | Create Date (Embedded) | Match? |
|---|---|---|---|
| dashboard_snap_1.jpg | 2026:04:13 23:47:08 | 2026:04:13 23:47:08 | ✅ Consistent |
| dashboard_snap_2.jpg | 2026:04:13 23:45:29 | 2026:04:13 23:47:08 | ❌ Impossible |

`dashboard_snap_2.jpg` has a `FileCreateDate` of `23:45:29` but an embedded `Create Date` of `23:47:08` — **1 minute 39 seconds in the future** relative to its own OS creation record.

This is physically impossible under normal conditions. A file's internal `Create Date` is written by the software that created it at the exact moment of creation. It cannot reflect a time that has not yet occurred at the point the file was created.

### What This Proves

The suspect used a timestomping tool on the night of April 13, 2026 in an attempt to manipulate the file timestamps — most likely to backdate them to January 2026 to support his cover story. The tool successfully altered the OS-level `FileCreateDate` and `FileModifyDate` values. However, it made a critical error — it wrote the **same embedded `Create Date` value into both files** instead of preserving their individual creation times.

This left `dashboard_snap_2.jpg` with an internal `Create Date` that is 1 minute 39 seconds ahead of its own OS `FileCreateDate` — a contradiction that is detectable only by examining both timestamp layers simultaneously, which is exactly what a full ExifTool dump reveals.

### The Three Timestamp Layers — Summary

| Layer | Type | Tamperable? | Both Files Show |
|---|---|---|---|
| `FileCreateDate` | OS-level | Yes — via timestomping tools | April 13, 2026 (inconsistent across files) |
| `Create Date` | Embedded in file binary | Harder — requires precise per-file editing | April 13, 2026 23:47:08 (identical on both — impossible) |
| `MetadataDate` | Embedded XMP metadata | Harder — requires XMP editor | April 13, 2026 (consistent with MAC times) |

All three layers confirm April 13, 2026. None support January 2026.

## Claim-by-Claim Analysis

### Claim 1 — "Screenshots were taken in January 2026"

**Status: Collapses Completely**

All three timestamp layers — OS-level MAC times, embedded `Create Date`, and `MetadataDate` — confirm both files were created on **April 13, 2026**. No timestamp anywhere in either file supports a January 2026 origin. The claim is false.

---

### Claim 2 — "Files have not been touched or accessed in months"

**Status: Collapses Completely**

`FileAccessDate` on both files shows **April 14, 2026 at 08:23 AM** — the morning of the seizure. `FileModifyDate` shows **April 13, 2026 at 23:45–23:47** — the night before. The files were actively accessed and modified within hours of the seizure, not months ago.

Furthermore, the timestomping evidence proves the suspect **deliberately interacted with these files** on the night of April 13th in an attempt to alter their metadata — the opposite of files left untouched for months.

## Formal Forensic Finding

> *Two screenshot files recovered from a hidden folder on the suspect's personal laptop were analyzed using ExifTool 13.59. Analysis contradicts the suspect's written statement on all counts. All timestamp layers — OS-level MAC times, embedded Create Date, and MetadataDate — confirm both files were created on April 13, 2026, not January 2026 as claimed. Both files were accessed on the morning of April 14, 2026, the day of seizure. A critical timestamp inconsistency was identified: `dashboard_snap_2.jpg` carries an embedded Create Date of 2026:04:13 23:47:08 while its OS-level FileCreateDate reads 2026:04:13 23:45:29 — a difference of 1 minute 39 seconds that is temporally impossible under normal file creation conditions. This inconsistency is consistent with the use of a timestomping tool that wrote identical embedded timestamps across both files while altering OS-level timestamps independently. The suspect appears to have attempted digital evidence tampering on the night of April 13, 2026 — less than 9 hours before the seizure — constituting a separate criminal charge of evidence tampering in addition to the original insider trading allegation.*

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Timestomping Tool Identification** — Forensically examine the suspect's laptop for recently installed or executed timestamp manipulation utilities. Browser history, recently run executables, and Prefetch files should be analyzed.

2. **USN Journal Analysis** — The Windows USN Journal on the suspect's laptop will contain a record of every file modification event. Cross-reference the journal entries for `dashboard_snap_1.jpg` and `dashboard_snap_2.jpg` against the timestamp anomalies found.

3. **Network Traffic Analysis** — Obtain ISP records for the suspect's home internet connection for April 13, 2026 between 23:00 and 24:00 to determine whether the screenshots were transmitted to any external party that night.

4. **Stockbroker Account Subpoena** — Obtain trading records from the alleged external stockbroker for the period following the financial projections captured in the screenshots.

5. **Full Disk Image Analysis** — Create a forensic image of the suspect's laptop using FTK Imager and analyze for deleted files, shadow copies, or remnants of the original unmanipulated screenshots.

## What This Demonstrates

This case exercise demonstrates timestomping detection — one of the most important anti-forensics techniques an investigator must be able to identify. It illustrates the critical difference between OS-level timestamps (which are relatively easy to manipulate) and embedded file-level timestamps (which are significantly harder to alter correctly). It also demonstrates why a full metadata dump is always necessary — selective tag extraction in Step 1 would have missed the `Create Date` contradiction entirely. The case reinforces a core forensic principle: an attacker who tampers with evidence under pressure almost always leaves a detectable trace.
