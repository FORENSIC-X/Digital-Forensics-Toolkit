## Case Background

A complaint was filed by a Delhi-based investigative journalist alleging that an unknown party was impersonating her online by fabricating defamatory documents under her name and distributing them to rival news outlets. Three `.docx` files purposedly authored by the journalist were forwarded to Delhi Police Cyber Crime Unit by the recipient outlets after the journalist denied authorship.

Three files were recovered for forensic analysis:

- `story_draft_1.docx`
- `story_draft_2.docx`
- `story_draft_3.docx`

## Findings

### Finding 1 — Author Identity Mismatch (Primary Finding)

**Tags:** `Creator`, `Last Modified By`

All three documents carry `Creator: Rajesh_WinPC` and `Last Modified By: Rajesh_WinPC`. The journalist's name appears nowhere in the metadata of any document.

This directly and unambiguously establishes that the journalist did not author these documents. Her statement is truthful and consistent with the metadata.

---

### Finding 2 — Windows Platform Contradiction

**Tag:** `Application: Microsoft Office Word 2019`

All three documents were created using **Microsoft Office Word 2019 on Windows**. Word for Mac and Word for Windows write different application strings into document metadata — it is not possible to produce a Windows application tag from a Mac machine.

The journalist stated she exclusively uses a MacBook Pro and has never used a Windows machine professionally. The metadata confirms this — her machine is physically incapable of generating this application tag.

**This finding independently destroys the defence argument that the journalist set up a fake account named `Rajesh_WinPC` on her own machine to frame someone.**

---

### Finding 3 — Cross-Device Transfer Evidence

**Tag:** `Zone Identifier: Exists`

All three files carry `Zone Identifier: Exists` — a Windows NTFS Alternate Data Stream tag that Windows automatically appends to any file received from an external source (downloaded from the internet, received via email, or transferred from an external device).

This confirms that none of the three documents were created directly on the machine where they were examined. They were created on a separate Windows machine and transferred to a secondary device before reaching the investigators.

Combined with Finding 2, this establishes that the documents originated on a Windows machine owned or operated by a party other than the journalist.

---

### Finding 4 — Timestamp Anomaly on story_draft_3.docx (Cross-Device Transfer Confirmation)

**Tags:** `File Create Date`, `Create Date`

| File | File Create Date (OS) | Create Date (Embedded) | Gap |
|---|---|---|---|
| story_draft_1.docx | 2026:05:10 23:12:39 | 2026:05:08 14:23:11 | Expected — different sessions |
| story_draft_2.docx | 2026:05:11 01:44:28 | 2026:05:09 19:45:02 | Expected — different sessions |
| story_draft_3.docx | 2026:05:11 02:31:12 | 2026:05:11 02:15:44 | **❌ Impossible** |

`story_draft_3.docx` has a `File Create Date` of `02:31:12` but an embedded `Create Date` of `02:15:44` — the OS-level timestamp is **15 minutes and 28 seconds ahead** of the embedded creation record.

A file's embedded `Create Date` is written by the application at the exact moment of document creation. It cannot reflect a time that occurs after the `File Create Date`. This is temporally impossible under normal single-machine conditions.

The most probable explanation: `story_draft_3.docx` was created on Machine A at `02:15:44` and transferred to Machine B at `02:31:12`. The embedded `Create Date` reflects the original creation on Machine A. The `File Create Date` reflects when the file arrived on Machine B. This confirms Rajesh operated across at least two devices during this campaign.

---

### Finding 5 — Behavioral Pattern Analysis

**Tags:** `Create Date`, `Total Edit Time`, `Words`

**Creation timeline across three days:**

| File | Created | Time of Day | Words | Edit Time |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| story_draft_1.docx | May 8, 2026 | 14:23 PM | 847 | 47 minutes |
| story_draft_2.docx | May 9, 2026 | 19:45 PM | 1203 | 83 minutes |
| story_draft_3.docx | May 11, 2026 | 02:15 AM | 412 | 15 minutes |

Two behavioral findings emerge:

**Sustained premeditation:** Documents were created across three consecutive days — May 8th, 9th, and 11th. This is a deliberate, sustained fabrication campaign, not a spontaneous act. The increasing word count from draft 1 to draft 2 suggests refinement and escalation.

**Rushed third document:** `story_draft_3.docx` — 412 words written in just 15 minutes at 2:15 AM. This is significantly faster than the pace of the first two documents and suggests the third document was written hastily, possibly under a deadline, possibly by copying and lightly editing content from the earlier drafts. The late-night creation time combined with the cross-device transfer anomaly suggests Rajesh was rushing to complete and distribute all three documents before morning.

## Anticipating and Destroying the Defence Argument

**Potential defence argument:** *"The journalist set up a fake Word account named Rajesh_WinPC on her own MacBook Pro to frame someone."*

This argument is destroyed by three independent layers of evidence:

**Layer 1 — Application tag:** Word for Mac cannot produce `Microsoft Office Word 2019` (Windows). The journalist's MacBook is physically incapable of generating this metadata regardless of what account name is configured.

**Layer 2 — Zone Identifier:** All three files carry `Zone Identifier: Exists`, proving they arrived on the examined machine from an external source. They were not created locally — they were created on a separate Windows machine and transferred.

**Layer 3 — Timestamp anomaly on story_draft_3.docx:** The 15-minute 28-second gap between the embedded `Create Date` and the OS `File Create Date` proves the file physically existed on one machine before appearing on another. This gap cannot be fabricated accidentally and is consistent only with cross-device file transfer.

Three independent findings, each individually sufficient, all corroborating each other.

## Formal Forensic Finding

> *Three Word documents submitted as evidence were analyzed using ExifTool 13.59. All three documents carry `Creator: Rajesh_WinPC` and `Last Modified By: Rajesh_WinPC` — the journalist's identity is absent from every document. All three were created using `Microsoft Office Word 2019` on Windows — a platform the journalist, by her own statement and the metadata evidence, does not use and whose application tag her MacBook Pro is physically incapable of generating. All three files carry `Zone Identifier: Exists`, confirming they were created on a separate Windows machine and transferred to the examined device. A timestamp anomaly on `story_draft_3.docx` — where the OS `File Create Date` of `02:31:12` is 15 minutes and 28 seconds ahead of the embedded `Create Date` of `02:15:44` — confirms cross-device file transfer and the use of at least two machines. Behavioral analysis of creation timestamps and edit times reveals a sustained three-day fabrication campaign with a rushed, late-night final document. The journalist's statement is fully corroborated by all metadata findings. The documents are attributed with high confidence to a Windows machine user operating under the account name `Rajesh_WinPC`.*

## Recommended Next Steps

1. **Device Identification** — Obtain a warrant to identify and seize the Windows machine associated with the username `Rajesh_WinPC`. Cross-reference with network logs from the journalist's building, office, or known associates to identify the physical device.

2. **Second Device Investigation** — Identify the secondary machine onto which `story_draft_3.docx` was transferred at `02:31 AM`. Network traffic logs or email server records from that timeframe may reveal the transfer mechanism (USB, email, cloud sync).

3. **Email and Distribution Trail** — Subpoena the rival news outlets for the email headers of the messages through which the fabricated documents were received. Email headers will reveal the originating IP address, email client, and potentially the sending device.

4. **Content Analysis** — Compare the linguistic style, formatting patterns, and structural similarities across all three documents to determine whether `story_draft_3.docx` was partially derived from the earlier two drafts — corroborating the rushed editing pattern finding.

5. **Cloud Storage Investigation** — If the Zone Identifier indicates internet download origin, subpoena relevant cloud storage providers (Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive) for account records associated with the upload of these files.

## Key Forensic Insight — Why Document Metadata Matters

`.docx` files embed a rich set of metadata that image files do not — including the author's Windows username, the exact application and version used, total editing time, revision count, and the full creation and modification timeline. This metadata is written automatically by Microsoft Word and cannot be suppressed without specialized tools. In document forgery and impersonation cases, this metadata is often the primary forensic evidence that establishes authorship and platform — making ExifTool as powerful on document files as it is on images.

## What This Demonstrates

This case exercise demonstrates document metadata forensics — the application of ExifTool to `.docx` files rather than images. It covers author identification via `Creator` and `Last Modified By` tags, platform verification via the `Application` tag, cross-device transfer detection via `Zone Identifier` and timestamp anomalies, and behavioral pattern analysis via `Create Date` and `Total Edit Time`. It also demonstrates the investigative discipline of anticipating defence arguments and layering evidence to pre-emptively collapse them — a skill as important as technical analysis in forensic practice.
